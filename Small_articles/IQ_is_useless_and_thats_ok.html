IQ is designed to be useless and that's OK

IQ is not the quite same thing as useful intelligence

Summary

IQ measures the ability to sit down and accurately solve a set of useless, uninteresting puzzles under time pressure. It is designed to do so for very good reasons. It is a good indicator for practically useful intelligence, but IQ does not determine your level of practically useful intelligence and thinking about IQ as a determiner of practical intelligence is probably hurting yourself and society. But let’s get a little bit more precise about this.

General and specific intelligence

Specific intelligence refers to the ability to perform some specific mental task, that might be the ability to order a set of numbers quickly or the ability to play chess competently or the ability to interpret a poem accurately. General intelligence on the other hand is supposed to measure one’s ability to do mental tasks across all possible domains. It can be approximated by averaging a wide range of specific types of intelligence, like verbal, numeric or spatial intelligence.

Charles Spearman was the first psychologist to propose this model of general and specific intelligence. He observed that a students performance on one subject, say maths, was positively correlated with that students performance in other subjects, say French, such that students who did good in maths on average had higher scores in all other subjects when compared to students who did poorly in maths. This pattern was found across all subjects. He imagined that everyone has a general intelligence g, which was like a starting point, but in addition he proposed that for each task there was a domain specific factor s which added or subtracted from this baseline to account for deviations (after all people don’t score the same grades in all subjects). So one’s ability to do maths for example would be the sum of that one person’s g-factor plus his subject specific factor s for maths and similarly for all other subjects or tasks.

There is some controversy as to how much you can train to improve g, if at all. However, it should not be controversial that the s-factor for any specific subject is completely trainable. After all, haven’t we all experienced this effect ourselves? We enter the education system ignorant of science, mathematics, art and philosophy and we come out having much more knowledge and ability. We have increased our subject specific intelligence in these areas by laborious study over many years. In just the same manner you could increase your ability in any mental activity. You get better at chess by attentively playing chess and thinking about the game, reading poetry makes you better at interpreting (and maybe writing) poems; and yes, taking IQ-tests very plausibly improves your ability to excel in IQ-tests.

Why IQ has to measure useless abilities

An IQ-test is supposed to measure g independently of the subjects specific factors s. If we want to measure the underlying g without also measuring the trainable s as a confounding factor, we have to ask questions that nobody trains for. Any type of question that frequently pops up in regular life will be open to distortion, due to some people having more experience and training in this task compared to others. Now most things we engage with fall either into the “useful” bucket or the “interesting” bucket. Useful things, like science, are commonly learned in school for obvious reasons. Interesting things are learned by many people just for fun, like chess or sudoku. But intelligence tests need to measure what we don’t commonly engage in, so their questions must be both uninteresting and useless.

So in a sense you could say that an IQ-test accurately measures g, precisely because the questions in an IQ-test are random, useless, uninteresting and thus are commonly not trained. If the questions were useful or interesting, they would be more commonly trained.

Some people don’t think that the questions on an IQ-test are useless, because the very fact that they are on an IQ test lends them credibility. Some people think (even if only subconsciously) “scientists use this to measure intelligence, so it must be important”. To show that this is emphatically not the case, it serves to go through an example. Now, I should say that I have never taken a professional IQ-test, so I don’t know if these questions are still used, but it is certainly the common perception that these kinds of questions are used in IQ-tests.

Obviously this is not how things work in the real world. Useful intelligence

Now the reason anybody cares about intelligence is because it is useful. For instance, if you are in a political debate, then intelligence helps you spot flaws in the reasoning of your opponent and it helps you to understand and explain arguments for your own position. Also all jobs require some specific types of intelligence to a greater or lesser degree. So intelligence matters because you can do things with it.

Obviously not all specific types of intelligence are created equal. The ability to play a game like chess (or to complete an IQ-test) is just for show, your ability to play chess never puts you in a life or death situation, you don’t get money from it, you don’t get rewards from it. [2]

In fact the sphere of useful intellectual skills is quite narrowly circumscribed: what is useful is being able to conform to and understand the eternal laws of nature found in mathematics, in science and in the humanities. Everything else is pretty much just following pointless rules we made up. To loosely quote Hume: if it is not about maths and not about anything in the real world, it is useless and a waste of time. [3]

This “useful intelligence” is also what we mean, if we talk about intelligence in real life. If you say one of your friend is really smart, then you don’t care about their ability to solve useless puzzles, you care about their ability to manipulate the environment and persuade other people.

Also despite what many people say about intelligence, they do care. Many people say that intelligence is not important at all, “IQ is just a number”, they say. But then tell them they are a 70-IQ idiot and they get offended, as if you have just wished death upon their whole family. Tell them they are a genius and they feel vindicated. We all know being smart is a big advantage. People care about intelligence, like it or not.

Useful intelligence is trainable

Now we can easily put the pieces together. It doesn’t take much time to increase s in one specific area of intelligence, and there are only a few types of intelligence that matter. So by all metrics that matter, you can get a lot smarter just by studying.

The belief that you can become better by applying yourself is called a growth mindset. Having a growth mindset about intelligence is a huge motivator for education. If you think that you can get smarter and better at understanding the world, you see your formal and informal education as meaningful. What better motivator is there, than to know that you are literally getting smarter (by the standards most commonly applied)?

You might not be increasing your general intelligence, but who cares about that? The only thing you need is a certain set of specific intelligences, if you can train those, you get all the benefits. [4]

Determinism about intelligence

However the people that talk a lot about IQ, tend to be very fixed-minded about intelligence.[5] People tend to be let down, when they think that g is unalterably determined by genetic factors outside of your control. They adopt a fatalistic doomer-mentality, where they might as well give up on learning, because they are never going to be as smart as their well-endowed peers. I don’t know, whether g is trainable or not, but even if the doomers are right and it isn’t trainable, you shouldn’t adopt the doomer-mentality. Whining in despair won’t make your problems go away, it’s just a net-negative for yourself and for society. You should just focus on getting as intelligent as you can. Adopting a growth mindset will help you make the most of what you have. Now use this motivation to read a difficult book or something.

People also point to certain correlations between IQ and income or wealth or life satisfaction. Those are true [6], but they shouldn’t affect your life in any meaningful way. These correlations are extremely weak, you can be dumber than 95% of the population and still be a multi-millionaire, or you can be a genius and still live on the streets. When studying society IQ has its uses; if IQ rises, that’s certainly a good thing, if it falls that’s probably a bad thing. But on the individual level it doesn’t really tell you much of anything. People are just tend to conflate a small correlation that becomes apparent in large data-sets, with a near omnipotent boogey-man, which determines whether you go to hell.

However, it is tremendously valuable for us to understand what intelligence is and even if IQ doesn’t capture all of intelligence, it would be foolish to simply dismiss IQ altogether. It will be the stepping stone for cognitive science to expand in the future and deepen our understanding about how we think. So the fact that IQ does not measure “useful” intelligence, in the sense outlined above, is no problem at all. I stand by all the points made above, but I don’t want to just hate on IQ. Some people say that IQ is just totally worthless for a concept, because they claim it was made up by eugenicists. I don’t want to be one of these people, so that’s why I’ve included the “that’s ok” in the title.

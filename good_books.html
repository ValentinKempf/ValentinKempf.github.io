Good Books

Here are all the books, that I can generally recommend. I only list books that I've read in full or in majority. So if you think one must-read is missing, then I probably just haven't read it (except for works that are older than 500 years, all of those that I have read are overrated pieces of absolute trash, especially Plato and Aristotle). Also some of the links are to the german original, since I don't want to recommend any specific translation that I haven't read.

You can also look at some of my book-reviews on goodreads.

Sometimes he projects his own terminology onto Hume instead of using the language of Hume himself, but that's the only thing I can criticize. -
Tom Clarke Encountering Naturalism Naturalism is a world view positing that only natural things exist in contrast to the supernatural. Tom Clark gives a strong manifesto, for why naturalism is a coherent world view and doesn't have to result in nihilism, like many religious people claim. On the contrary, naturalism is among the most meaningful of all world views. Naturalism.org
Peter Singer The expanding circle This book is about the foundations of ethics and about its relations to biology. It engages especially thoroughly with E.O.Wilson, whose work is built upon, but also critiqued. This book circumvents the pitfalls that are common to most people, when they talk about this topic (1) purely from philosophy or (2) purely from biology. Singer synthesizes and harmonizes both perspectives. The expanding circle is a metaphor, which stands for the circle of persons who are worthy of moral consideration, which seems to be growing across history, starting with our closest relatives, going over to ethnicity and humanity (and maybe in the future to all mammals or vertebrates). Although this metaphor lends this book its title, it only appears in the last third of the book, and isn't even very prominent there. What I'm saying is: the title is not very fitting, but the rest is still good stuffs. -

Konrad Lorenz King Solomon's Ring Interesting stories about the behavior of animals: dogs, geese, fish, jackdaws, chimpanzee and more. Even besides the fact, that you learn much about different animals and come to see the world from their perspective, this work can also be seen as a collection of short stories, with good literary quality, which can be read for pleasure -

Kants Lehre vom apriorischen im Lichte gegenwärtiger Biologie To what extent can evolution tell us something, about our a priori faculties of reason? As far as I know, not translated. Link to pdf (German)
Vergleichende Verhaltensforschung: Grundlagen der Ethologie Philosophy of science (specially geared towards ethology), theoretical and technical summary of the insights found in the stories of previous books. -
Behind the Mirror: A Search for a Natural History of Human Knowledge Similar to the last entry, but the first chapter is a lot more philosophical. If the first chapter seems difficult don't get discouraged, it's going to get easier after chapter 1. -
Note on Konrad Lorenz He has also wrote two books, which I haven't read, but where I am pretty sure that they are garbage. That being civilized man's eight deadly sins and The Waning of Humaneness . I don't want to totally discourage anybody from reading these, since I haven't read them myself, but to me they give me way too much of the „old religious man yells at cloud“-vibe. Also the last chapters of On Aggression covered similar grounds and they were already pretty garbage. I'm noting this, since it might seem like I endorse all of Lorenz's works. -
Frans de Waal Are we smart enough to know how smart animals are? On intelligent behavior in animals. The title sounds a little philosophical, but the book is about concrete examples of intelligent behavior, more than how to assess intelligent behavior -
The Bonobo and the Atheist A book about the origins of morality in emotional thinking. There are many lines of evidence that morality is no top-down construction rationalized by philosophy or religion. Morality is something that comes from within and is emotionally anchored in us and is constructed bottom-up. The best proof of this is that primates have their own kind of morality, Bonobos and Chimpanzees treat their conspecifics with respect, even when they fight with one another, this happens in a social context according to moral rules and with the background knowledge that the suffering of others is to be accounted for. Through this it becomes clear that religion is not necessary for a morally healthy society. -
Richard Dawkins The Selfish Gene Classic of evolutionary biology, very accessible and easy to read. You could honestly read it before bed. -
The extended phenotype Continues the thoughts from The Selfish Gene. It is a little bit more academic than its predecessor, but it is still more accessible than most other writings on evolution. -
The genetic book of the dead Many repetitions from the last two books, but some things have changed in the almost 50 years between this book and the last two. In terms of difficulty it's somewhere in between those two. -
Ursula Goodenough The Sacred Depths of Nature A popular science book with a focus on the religious feelings that can stem from a deep understanding of science. Topics covered: formations of stars, abiogenesis, reductionism, what is an organism, evolution, biodiversity, consciousness, emotions, sex, intimacy, death, human evolution, morality. -
Ed Yong An immense world A journey through the sensory worlds (or Umwelten) of all kinds of different animals . -
Nicholas P. Money The Amoeba in the Room: Lives of the Microbes I thought the title was exaggerated, but by the end of the book, I think you will be convinced that the vast majority of the action on earth is happening in the microbiosphere. It would be one thing, if we were actually missing the elephant in the room, but what's even worse is that we are truly missing the amoeba in the room.Very easy book, can be finished in a day or two. -

Self-help

Simone Collins, Malcolm Collins The Pragmatist’s Guide to Life: A Guide to Creating Your Own Answers to Life’s Biggest Questions The most important choice in life, is what you want to strive towards. Without a clear idea of what you want to achieve in life you will be pushed around by the whims of emotion and cultural conditioning. You can choose anything you like as your goal, happiness, fulfillment, notoriety, spiritual enlightenment etc. This book helps you sharpen your idea of a life's goal and gives you guidance in how this should influence your actions. This should basically be manditory for anybody trying to do self-improvement, as without clear goals self-improvement is unlikely to stick. Their website
Kelly McGonigal The Upside of Stress: Why Stress Is Good for You and How to Get Good at it Teaches the proper mindset of how to deal with and think about adversities in life without getting overwhelmed unnecessarily. -

Spirituality

Sam Harris Waking Up: A Guide to Spirituality Without Religion Probably the best place to start with meditation, if you are skeptical and feel like there are many superstitions caught up in the world of spirituality. Sam Harris is a militant atheist, and is not afraid to mock religions as non-sense from the iron age (sic) even in this book. So his skeptical take on the core of meditation grounded in the philosophy of mind is sure to be persuasive. -
Daniel Goleman, Richard J. Davidson Altered Traits: Science Reveals How Meditation Changes Your Mind, Brain and Body Another good place to start with meditation. Most of the claims are backed up by hard scientific data, and those that aren't are marked as such. They are more open to supposed knowledge found in ancient traditions, though only for the finding of hypotheses. -
Leigh Brasington Right concentration: a practical guide to the jhanas A guide for deeply focused meditations. You should probably have a couple dozen hours of meditation under your belt before looking at these techniques, though it will not hurt to look at them, even if you are just beginning. In the second part he gives some pretty accessible critical scholarship on what the suttas (the oldest writings in buddhism) say about the jhanas and show how they are internally self-contradictory due to their editorial history. Curiously he does not make the obvious jump of saying: "because of that the suttas are not generally reliable sources", which is obviously the rational move. The suttas contain contradictions, just as every holy text of every major religion. Leigh Brasington's website
Tatjana Schnell Psychologie des Lebenssinns Psychological findings about the causes and the effects of felt meaning in life. This book really has the potential to change how you think about meaning in life (and to find more meaning in your own life). Springer
